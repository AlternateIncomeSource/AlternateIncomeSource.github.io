What Your WPM Score Actually Means

The average adult types between 38 and 45 WPM. Reaching 60 WPM puts you above average. Most professional typists sit between 75 and 100 WPM. This test shows you both gross WPM (everything you typed) and net WPM adjusted for errors — because net WPM is what matters in real-world use and in professional typing assessments.

How to Improve Your Typing Speed

The single most effective habit is to prioritise accuracy over speed. Typing fast but incorrectly trains your fingers to make the same mistakes repeatedly. Start with the free lesson system, which begins at home-row basics and builds progressively through 27 structured lessons across four levels. Daily practice of 10 to 15 minutes is more effective than occasional longer sessions. Speed follows accuracy naturally — not the other way around.

What's a Good Typing Speed? (By Skill Level and Job)

Typing speed is usually grouped into rough bands rather than one fixed number, since it depends heavily on practice and how someone learned to type in the first place:

  • Beginner: 10–20 WPM — still finding keys, mostly without touch typing
  • Average adult typist: 38–45 WPM — the typical range for someone who types daily but was never formally trained
  • Above average: 60+ WPM — comfortable touch typing, few corrections needed
  • Professional typist: 65–75 WPM — the common baseline for administrative and data-heavy roles
  • Advanced / specialist: 80–100+ WPM — experienced data entry and transcription professionals
  • Elite / competitive typists: 120+ WPM — a small minority, usually with years of deliberate practice

By job type, commonly expected minimums look like this: general office and administrative roles typically ask for 40–60 WPM; data entry specialist roles usually require 50–70 WPM at 95% or higher accuracy; legal and medical transcription often expects 65–80 WPM; executive assistant roles commonly sit at 60–80 WPM. These are typical ranges seen in job postings, not fixed rules — some employers set the bar lower for training positions and higher for senior ones.

WPM vs CPM: What's the Difference?

WPM (Words Per Minute) is the standard used across almost every typing test, including this one. By convention, one "word" equals 5 characters, including spaces — this keeps the measurement consistent regardless of whether the text you typed happened to use short or long words. CPM (Characters Per Minute) is the raw character count typed per minute, with no averaging applied. As a rule of thumb, CPM is roughly WPM × 5. WPM is more useful for comparing your result against typical benchmarks and job requirements, since almost every published typing benchmark uses WPM. CPM can be more informative if you're typing in a language where average word length differs significantly from English.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is this typing test completely free?
Yes. Every feature — speed tests, all 27 lessons, history, achievements — is free. No account, no payment, no ads interrupting your test. Your results are stored privately in your own browser and never sent anywhere.
How is WPM calculated?
One word in a typing test equals 5 characters including spaces. Gross WPM divides your total character count by 5 then by time in minutes. Net WPM subtracts one WPM for every error you made per minute. This test displays both so you can see the real cost of mistakes on your score.
What is a good typing speed in WPM?
38–45 WPM is average for adults who never learned formal touch typing. 60+ WPM is considered above average. Most professional typing roles expect 65–75 WPM, and experienced data entry specialists often reach 80–100+ WPM.
What is the difference between WPM and CPM?
WPM (Words Per Minute) counts every 5 characters, including spaces, as one word — it's the standard used by almost all typing tests and job requirements. CPM (Characters Per Minute) is the raw character count with no averaging. CPM is roughly WPM multiplied by 5.
What typing speed do I need for data entry jobs?
Most data entry roles require 45 to 60 WPM with 95 percent or higher accuracy. Some specialist positions ask for 70 to 80 WPM. This test tracks both metrics and shows exactly where accuracy is dropping so you can target the right practice.
Can I use this to prepare for a job typing test?
Yes. Practice with the 60-second and 120-second modes to match the duration of most employment typing assessments. Aim for 95 percent or higher accuracy at your target WPM before applying, and use the lesson system to fix your weakest keys specifically.
How long does it take to learn touch typing?
Most people can learn solid home-row fundamentals within one to two weeks of daily 10–15 minute practice, and reach a comfortable, accurate 40+ WPM within four to eight weeks. Reaching 70+ WPM typically takes several months of regular use, since speed comes from repetition, not from any single technique.
Does the RGB keyboard affect my typing?
The visual keyboard is a feedback tool, not a distraction. It shows which key you just pressed and whether it was correct — reinforcing the connection between your fingers and the keys. For beginners this visual confirmation accelerates learning considerably.
Does this work on mobile?
Yes. The test, results, and full lesson system all work on phones and tablets using your device's own keyboard. The on-screen RGB keyboard is a desktop feature and is hidden on small screens since your device keyboard takes its place.
